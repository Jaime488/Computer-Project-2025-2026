WW1 (1914-1918)

A7V

The A7V was Germany's first operational tank during World War I, entering service in 1918. It had a boxy, rectangular shape with armor up to 30 mm thick, offering some protection against small arms and shrapnel. Its main armament was a 57 mm Maxim-Nordenfelt gun, supplemented by multiple 7.92 mm MG08 machine guns for infantry defense. Powered by a 200 hp engine, it could reach speeds of about 15 km/h on roads, but struggled over rough terrain due to its weight and limited suspension. Only about 20 units were built, making it rare and largely a symbolic entry into Germany's armored warfare development.

A7V-U

The A7V-U was a German experimental tank variant developed during World War I as an improvement on the original A7V. Unlike the standard A7V, it featured fully tracked, rhomboid-like hulls similar to British tanks, which improved mobility over rough terrain and trenches. It was heavily armored, designed to withstand small arms and light artillery fire, and retained multiple machine guns for defense against infantry. The vehicle was significantly larger and heavier than the original A7V, which made it difficult to transport and mechanically unreliable. Only a prototype was built, and it never saw combat before the war ended.

K-Wagen

The K-Wagen was a massive German super-heavy tank concept developed near the end of World War I. It was designed to carry a main armament of four 77 mm guns along with multiple machine guns, mounted in a huge, armored hull to break through enemy lines. The tank was extremely heavy, around 120 tons, and powered by two 240 hp engines, giving it very limited mobility of about 8 km/h. Its design included a rhomboid shape with tracks running the full length of the vehicle, intended to cross trenches and rough terrain. Only two prototypes were partially built, and neither saw combat before the war ended.

LK-I

The LK I (Leichter Kampfwagen I) was an early German light tank prototype developed in 1918 during World War I. It was designed to be smaller and more mobile than the massive A7V, with armor up to 8 mm thick and a weight of about 6.75 tons. Its armament consisted of a single 37mm cannon and a machine gun, intended for infantry support. The tank had tracked mobility and could reach speeds up to 14 km/h on roads, making it more versatile in rough terrain than heavier tanks. Only a few prototypes were built, and it never saw combat before the war ended.

LK-II

The LK II (Leichter Kampfwagen II) was the successor to the LK I, developed by Germany in 1918 as a light tank for faster, more maneuverable operations. It was slightly larger and better armored than the LK I, with armor up to 14 mm thick and a weight of around 8 tons. Its main armament was a 37 mm cannon, complemented by a machine gun for infantry defense. The LK II had improved suspension and tracked mobility, allowing it to reach speeds of up to 18 km/h, making it suitable for reconnaissance and supporting infantry. Only a few prototypes were built, and it did not see combat before the end of World War I.

Interwar & WW2 (1919-1945)

Grosstraktor

The Grosstraktor was an early German tank prototype developed during World War I to provide armored support to infantry. It was a very large, tractor-like vehicle with heavy armor designed to cross trenches and rough terrain. Its armament included a machine gun and a small cannon, though it was primarily intended to tow artillery and supplies rather than engage directly in combat. Powered by a gasoline engine, it was slow and mechanically unreliable, limiting its operational usefulness. Only a few prototypes were built, and it never saw action on the battlefield.

Panzer I

The Panzer I was Germany's first mass-produced tank, introduced in the early 1930s as a light training and reconnaissance vehicle. It was lightly armored, with protection mainly against small arms fire, and weighed about 5.4 tons. Its armament consisted of two machine guns, making it unsuitable for engaging other tanks. The Panzer I was fast and highly maneuverable for its time, reaching speeds of up to 37 km/h on roads. It saw action in the Spanish Civil War and early World War II campaigns, mainly in support roles rather than frontline tank battles.

Panzer II

The Panzer II was a German light tank developed in the 1930s to supplement the Panzer I with slightly better firepower and armor. It weighed around 8 to 9 tons and had armor capable of withstanding small arms and light anti-tank weapons. Its main armament was a 20 mm autocannon, supported by a machine gun, making it effective against infantry and lightly armored vehicles. The tank was relatively fast, reaching speeds of about 40 km/h, and was highly maneuverable on roads and rough terrain. Panzer II saw extensive use in the early stages of World War II, including the invasions of Poland and France, before being gradually replaced by more heavily armed tanks.

Panzer III

The Panzer III was a medium German tank developed in the late 1930s to engage other armored vehicles and support infantry. It weighed around 23 tons and had armor up to 70 mm thick in later versions, providing decent protection against early-war anti-tank weapons. Its main armament evolved from a 37 mm to a 50 mm cannon, and it carried a coaxial machine gun for additional firepower. The tank could reach speeds of about 40 km/h and was known for its reliability and good mobility on various terrains. Panzer III was widely used during the early and mid-stages of World War II, including campaigns in Poland, France, and North Africa, before being gradually replaced by the Panzer IV and heavier tanks.

Panzer IV

The Panzer IV was a German medium tank introduced in the late 1930s and became one of the most widely used tanks of World War II. It initially carried a short-barreled 75 mm gun for infantry support, but later versions were upgraded with a long-barreled 75 mm gun capable of taking on other tanks. Its armor ranged from 30 mm to 80 mm, providing solid protection against early- and mid-war anti-tank weapons. Weighing around 25 tons, it could reach speeds of about 40 km/h and was highly versatile across different terrains. The Panzer IV saw action on all fronts during the war and remained in production throughout, adapting to evolving battlefield requirements.

Marder I

The Marder I was a German tank destroyer developed during World War II, built on captured or obsolete French tank chassis. It was lightly armored, offering protection mainly against small arms fire, and carried a powerful 75 mm anti-tank gun capable of engaging most Allied tanks at medium range. Its open-topped design made it vulnerable to artillery and infantry attacks but allowed for a lighter vehicle and better gun elevation. The Marder I weighed around 10–12 tons and had reasonable mobility on roads and rough terrain. It was used primarily on the Eastern Front to counter Soviet armor during the early years of the war.

Wespe

The Wespe was a German self-propelled artillery vehicle used during World War II, based on a modified Panzer II chassis. It was equipped with a 105 mm howitzer, providing mobile artillery support for German armored divisions. Its light armor protected the crew from small arms fire and shell fragments but left it vulnerable to direct hits. The Wespe weighed around 11 tons and had good mobility, allowing it to keep pace with fast-moving Panzer units. It was primarily deployed on the Eastern and Western Fronts, offering indirect fire support for infantry and tanks.

Tiger I

The Tiger I was a German heavy tank introduced in 1942, designed to dominate the battlefield with superior firepower and armor. It weighed around 57 tons and had armor up to 100 mm thick, making it nearly impervious to most Allied tank guns early in its service. Its main armament was the 88 mm KwK 36 gun, capable of destroying enemy tanks at long range, supported by two machine guns. Despite its heavy weight, it could reach speeds of about 38 km/h on roads, though it was less mobile off-road and mechanically demanding. The Tiger I saw action on all major fronts from 1942 to 1945 and became one of the most feared tanks of World War II.

Panther I

The Panther I was a German medium tank introduced in 1943 to counter the Soviet T-34 and combine firepower, armor, and mobility. It weighed around 44 tons and had sloped armor up to 80 mm thick, which provided excellent protection against most Allied anti-tank weapons. Its main armament was a 75 mm high-velocity gun, effective at long range against enemy armor, supported by coaxial and hull machine guns. The tank could reach speeds of about 46 km/h on roads and had good cross-country mobility for its size. Panther I was widely used on the Eastern and Western Fronts and became one of Germany's most capable and iconic tanks of World War II.

Hummel

The Hummel was a German self-propelled artillery vehicle used during World War II, built on a modified Panzer III/IV chassis. It was armed with a 150 mm howitzer for indirect fire support, providing long-range artillery for armored and infantry units. The vehicle had relatively light armor, enough to protect the crew from small arms fire and shell fragments but vulnerable to direct hits. Weighing around 24 tons, it had good mobility for a vehicle of its size, allowing it to keep pace with Panzer divisions. The Hummel was primarily deployed on the Eastern and Western Fronts, offering mobile artillery fire where conventional towed guns would have been less effective.

Jagdpanther

The Jagdpanther was a German tank destroyer introduced in 1944, based on the Panther chassis. It combined strong frontal armor with a powerful 88 mm gun capable of destroying most Allied tanks at long range. Its sloped armor design improved protection while keeping weight manageable at around 45 tons. The Jagdpanther had good mobility for its size, reaching speeds of about 46 km/h on roads. It was primarily used on the Eastern and Western Fronts to ambush enemy armor, making it one of Germany's most effective tank destroyers of World War II.

Tiger II

The Tiger II, also known as the King Tiger, was a German heavy tank introduced in 1944. It weighed around 69 tons and had very thick, sloped armor—up to 180 mm on the front—making it extremely difficult to penetrate. Its main armament was an 88 mm KwK 43 gun, capable of destroying most Allied tanks at long range. Despite its firepower and protection, the Tiger II was slow and mechanically complex, with a top speed of about 38 km/h and frequent maintenance issues. It saw action on both the Eastern and Western Fronts, becoming one of the most formidable tanks of World War II.

Maus

The Maus was a German super-heavy tank developed during World War II, intended to be virtually invulnerable to enemy fire. It weighed around 188 tons, making it the heaviest tank ever built, and had extremely thick armor—up to 240 mm in places. Its main armament consisted of a 128 mm gun, supported by a coaxial 75 mm gun, designed to destroy any Allied vehicle. The tank was very slow, with a top speed of about 20 km/h on roads, and suffered from severe mobility and logistical limitations due to its size. Only two prototypes were completed, and the Maus never saw combat.

Jagdtiger

The Jagdtiger was a German heavy tank destroyer introduced in 1944, built on the Tiger II chassis. It weighed around 72 tons and had extremely thick armor, up to 250 mm at the front, making it nearly impervious to most Allied tank guns. Its main armament was a powerful 128 mm gun, capable of destroying any Allied tank at long range. Despite its firepower and protection, the Jagdtiger was very slow, with a top speed of about 34 km/h, and suffered from mechanical reliability issues. Only around 80 units were produced, and they saw limited action on both the Western and Eastern Fronts.

Cold War (1946-1991)

Leopard 1

The Leopard 1 was a German main battle tank introduced in the 1960s, designed to emphasize mobility and firepower over heavy armor. It weighed around 42 tons and had relatively light armor compared to contemporary tanks, relying on speed and maneuverability for protection. Its main armament was a 105 mm Royal Ordnance L7 gun, effective against most tanks of its era, supported by coaxial and roof-mounted machine guns. The tank could reach speeds of about 65 km/h on roads, giving it excellent operational flexibility. The Leopard 1 was widely exported and remained in service for several decades, forming the basis for later German tank development.

Leopard 2 (Proto)

The Leopard 2 prototype was a German main battle tank developed in the 1970s as a successor to the Leopard 1. It weighed around 55 to 60 tons and featured significantly improved armor protection, including composite armor to withstand contemporary anti-tank weapons. Its main armament was a 105 mm or experimental 120 mm gun, capable of engaging modern armored threats at long range. The tank retained high mobility for its weight, reaching speeds of about 65 km/h on roads and demonstrating good cross-country performance. The Leopard 2 prototype served as the foundation for the production Leopard 2 series, which became one of the most successful and widely used main battle tanks in the world.

Leopard 2

The Leopard 2 is a German main battle tank introduced in the late 1970s as a successor to the Leopard 1, designed to combine firepower, protection, and mobility. It weighs around 60 tons and features advanced composite armor, providing strong protection against modern anti-tank weapons. Its main armament is a 120 mm smoothbore gun, capable of firing a variety of ammunition types, supported by coaxial and roof-mounted machine guns. The tank can reach speeds of about 68 km/h on roads and has excellent cross-country mobility. The Leopard 2 has been continually upgraded over the decades and remains one of the most widely used and respected main battle tanks in the world.

1992-Present

Leopard 2A5

The Leopard 2A5 is an upgraded version of the Leopard 2 main battle tank, introduced in the late 1990s to improve protection and battlefield survivability. It features a redesigned, angular turret with spaced and sloped composite armor, providing enhanced defense against kinetic and shaped-charge projectiles. Its main armament is a 120 mm smoothbore gun, capable of firing advanced armor-piercing and high-explosive rounds, supported by coaxial and roof-mounted machine guns. The tank weighs around 62 tons and maintains a top speed of about 68 km/h, with excellent mobility for a vehicle of its size. The Leopard 2A5 has been widely deployed and served as the basis for further modernizations in the Leopard 2 series.

KF51 Panther

The Rheinmetall KF51 Panther is a next-generation German main battle tank introduced in 2022, designed to enhance firepower, mobility, and survivability on the modern battlefield. It features a 130 mm Rheinmetall Future Gun System (FGS) with an autoloader capable of firing advanced munitions, offering a 50% longer effective range than the standard 120 mm guns on current MBTs. The tank is equipped with a multi-layered protection system, including passive, reactive, and active armor, along with Rheinmetall's StrikeShield active defense system and Top Attack Protection System (TAPS) to counter various threats. Powered by a 1,475-horsepower diesel engine, the KF51 Panther achieves a top speed of 70 km/h and boasts a range exceeding 500 km, providing excellent mobility. Designed for a three-person crew, it incorporates a fully digitized Next Generation Vehicle Architecture (NGVA) for enhanced situational awareness and networked operations.
