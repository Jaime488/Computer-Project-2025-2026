WW1 (1914-1918)

Tsar Tank

The Tsar Tank, also called the Lebedenko Tank, was an experimental Russian armored vehicle built in 1914–1915. It featured a highly unusual design with two giant front wheels over 9 meters in diameter and a small rear wheel for balance, resembling a tricycle. The concept was intended to allow the tank to easily roll over obstacles and trenches on the battlefield. However, during testing, the rear wheel sank into soft ground, leaving the vehicle immobile, and the massive wheels made it an easy target. Due to these flaws, the Tsar Tank was abandoned and never entered production.

Vezdekhod

The Vezdekhod (“goes anywhere”) was a Russian prototype built in 1915, often considered one of the world's first tank concepts. It was designed by Aleksandr Porokhovschikov as a small, light, tracked vehicle powered by a 10-horsepower engine. Unlike the massive Tsar Tank, the Vezdekhod used a single wide track that wrapped around its body, with small wheels on the sides for steering. Its purpose was to cross trenches and rough terrain while carrying either weapons or supplies. Although tested, it suffered from poor performance and stability issues, and the Russian military rejected it in favor of later armored car and tank designs.

Interwar & WW2 (1919-1945)

T-26

The T-26 was a Soviet light infantry tank developed in the early 1930s, based on the British Vickers 6-Ton design. It became one of the most widely produced tanks of the interwar period, with over 10,000 built. Armed primarily with a 45 mm gun and a machine gun, it was lightly armored but effective against infantry and early armored vehicles. The T-26 saw extensive combat in conflicts such as the Spanish Civil War, the Winter War against Finland, and the early stages of World War II. By 1941, however, it was obsolete against newer German tanks and suffered heavy losses, leading to its eventual replacement by more advanced Soviet designs like the T-34.

BT-2

The BT-2 was the Soviet Union's first model in the BT (“Bystrokhodny Tank” or “fast tank”) series, entering service in 1932. It was based on the American Christie tank chassis, which allowed it to run on either tracks or wheels, giving it exceptional speed for its time. Armed with a 37 mm gun and a machine gun, it had relatively light armor, prioritizing mobility over protection. The BT-2 was used mainly for training and early mechanized units, though some saw limited combat in the early 1930s. Its design paved the way for later BT models, which eventually influenced the legendary T-34.

T-28

The T-28 was a Soviet multi-turreted medium tank developed in the early 1930s and first produced in 1933. It featured a large central turret armed with a 76.2 mm gun and two smaller machine gun turrets at the front, giving it a distinctive and heavily armed appearance. Designed to break through fortified enemy positions, it was among the first medium tanks in the world. The T-28 saw combat during the Soviet invasion of Poland in 1939, the Winter War with Finland, and the early stages of World War II. However, its relatively thin armor and mechanical unreliability led to high losses, and it was eventually replaced by more modern tanks like the T-34.

T-34

The T-34 was the Soviet Union's most famous and influential tank of World War II, first entering service in 1940. It featured a revolutionary combination of sloped armor, mobility, and firepower, making it highly effective against early German tanks. Initially armed with a 76.2 mm gun (later upgraded to an 85 mm), it balanced speed, protection, and ease of production better than most contemporaries. The T-34 played a decisive role in battles such as the defense of Moscow, Stalingrad, and the Battle of Kursk, often being credited with turning the tide on the Eastern Front. With over 80,000 built, it became one of the most produced and iconic tanks in history, influencing postwar tank design worldwide.

KV-1

The KV-1 was a Soviet heavy tank introduced in 1939, named after Kliment Voroshilov. It was heavily armored for its time, with up to 90 mm of protection, making it nearly invulnerable to most German anti-tank weapons during the early stages of Operation Barbarossa. Armed with a 76.2 mm gun, it combined strong firepower with thick armor but suffered from being slow, mechanically unreliable, and difficult to maneuver. Despite its drawbacks, it shocked the German Army in 1941, as few of their weapons could stop it head-on. By 1943, however, advances in German tanks and anti-tank guns made the KV-1 obsolete, and it was gradually replaced by the more effective IS-series heavy tanks.

KV-2

The KV-2 was a Soviet heavy assault tank developed in 1940 as a variant of the KV-1. It was distinguished by its massive, boxy turret mounting a 152 mm howitzer, designed for destroying bunkers and fortifications. While its firepower was formidable, the tank was extremely heavy, slow, and mechanically unreliable, with the oversized turret making it a large and easy target. During the German invasion in 1941, KV-2s initially terrified enemy forces, as their thick armor and powerful gun could overwhelm most German weapons. However, their poor mobility, fuel consumption, and vulnerability to breakdowns meant they were soon phased out, with only a limited number ever produced.

SU-85

The SU-85 was a Soviet self-propelled gun and tank destroyer introduced in 1943 during World War II. Built on the chassis of the T-34 medium tank, it mounted an 85 mm D-5T gun in a fixed casemate superstructure, giving it the ability to engage and destroy newer German tanks like the Panther and Tiger at longer ranges. It sacrificed a turret for stronger armament and a lower silhouette, making it more effective in ambush and defensive roles. The SU-85 proved valuable in restoring the Red Army's ability to counter heavily armored German vehicles until the introduction of the T-34/85 tank. By 1944, it was gradually replaced by the more powerful SU-100, which carried a 100 mm gun.

SU-122

The SU-122 was a Soviet self-propelled howitzer introduced in late 1942 during World War II. Built on the T-34 chassis, it mounted a 122 mm M-30S howitzer in a fixed casemate, designed to provide direct fire support against fortifications, infantry, and light vehicles. Its large high-explosive shells were effective against enemy strongpoints and could even damage German heavy tanks through concussive force, though it was not primarily intended as a tank destroyer. The SU-122 was produced in limited numbers and saw combat on the Eastern Front, often operating alongside T-34s to break through defensive positions. By 1944, it was largely replaced by more specialized vehicles like the SU-85 and SU-100, which were better suited for anti-tank roles.

IS-2

The IS-2 (Iosif Stalin tank) was a Soviet heavy tank introduced in 1943 as a successor to the KV series. It featured thick sloped armor and was armed with a powerful 122 mm D-25T gun, capable of destroying German Tigers and Panthers as well as fortifications. Despite its relatively slow reload time, its firepower and protection made it a formidable breakthrough tank. The IS-2 played a major role in the Red Army's offensives from 1944 onward, including the Battle of Berlin. Its design influenced postwar Soviet heavy tanks and cemented its reputation as one of the most powerful tanks of World War II.

SU-100

The SU-100 was a Soviet tank destroyer introduced in 1944, built on the proven T-34 chassis as a successor to the SU-85. It mounted a powerful 100 mm D-10S gun, which could penetrate the armor of German heavy tanks like the Panther and Tiger I, and even threaten the Tiger II under the right conditions. With its low silhouette, strong frontal armor, and long-range firepower, the SU-100 was highly effective in both defensive and offensive roles. It saw extensive service during the final year of World War II, including in the push toward Berlin. After the war, the SU-100 remained in use for decades, serving in many countries and conflicts around the world.

SU-152

The SU-152 was a Soviet self-propelled heavy assault gun introduced in 1943 during World War II. Built on the KV-1S heavy tank chassis, it was armed with a massive 152 mm howitzer, designed to smash German fortifications and bunkers. Its huge shells also proved devastating against German heavy tanks like the Tiger and Panther, earning it the nickname “Zveroboy” (Beast Slayer). While extremely powerful, it had drawbacks such as a slow rate of fire, limited ammunition capacity, and a heavy, unwieldy design. The SU-152 played a key role in battles like Kursk and laid the foundation for later heavy SPGs such as the ISU-152.

IS-3

The IS-3 (Iosif Stalin 3) was a Soviet heavy tank unveiled in 1945, just before the end of World War II. It featured a distinctive rounded “pike nose” glacis and a hemispherical turret, giving it excellent ballistic protection and influencing postwar tank design worldwide. Armed with a 122 mm gun, it was capable of defeating heavy German armor, though it saw little to no action before the war ended. Its appearance at the Berlin Victory Parade in September 1945 shocked Western observers, as it looked far more advanced than Allied tanks of the time. The IS-3 went on to serve for decades, receiving upgrades, and remained a symbol of Soviet heavy tank power during the early Cold War.

Cold War (1946-1991)

T-54

The T-54 was a Soviet medium tank developed right after World War II, entering production in 1947. Designed as the successor to the T-34 and T-44, it featured sloped armor, a 100 mm D-10T gun, and improved mobility, making it one of the most advanced tanks of its era. Its balance of firepower, protection, and ease of mass production led to it becoming the backbone of the Soviet and Warsaw Pact armored forces. The T-54 (and later its improved T-55 variant) was produced in the tens of thousands, making it the most produced tank in history. It saw combat across the world during the Cold War and remains in service with some nations even today.

SU-100M

The SU-100M was a Soviet prototype self-propelled gun developed shortly after World War II as part of efforts to modernize wartime designs. It was based on the SU-100, but instead of the T-34 chassis, it used the newer T-54 chassis, giving it better protection, mobility, and reliability. Armed with the same 100 mm D-10S gun, it retained strong anti-tank capabilities while benefiting from improved armor layout and a lower silhouette. Despite promising features, the SU-100M never entered mass production, as Soviet doctrine was shifting toward turreted tanks like the T-54 and later MBTs. However, it represented an important transitional step between wartime self-propelled guns and postwar armored vehicles.

T-10

The T-10 was the last Soviet heavy tank, entering service in 1953 as a direct successor to the IS-series. It featured improved sloped armor, a powerful 122 mm M-62 gun, and better mobility compared to earlier IS tanks. Unlike the IS-3 with its distinctive pike nose, the T-10 had a longer hull and a more conventional but heavily armored design. It served as the backbone of Soviet heavy tank units during the early Cold War, but by the 1960s, the heavy tank concept was being phased out in favor of more versatile main battle tanks like the T-62. The T-10 remained in limited service for decades and is considered the final evolution of the Soviet heavy tank line.

Obj. 279

The Object 279 was an experimental Soviet heavy tank developed in 1957, designed to operate in extreme battlefield conditions, including nuclear warfare. It had a very unusual elliptical, UFO-like hull shape with thick sloped armor, intended to resist nuclear blast waves and prevent overturning. Armed with a 130 mm M-65 rifled gun and equipped with an autoloader, it was one of the most heavily armed and armored tanks of its era. Its unique four-track suspension gave it excellent mobility on soft ground like snow and swamps, though it was extremely complex and difficult to maintain. Only a single prototype was built, as Soviet doctrine shifted toward main battle tanks, making the Object 279 the last of the true heavy tank experiments.

T-55

The T-55 was a Soviet medium tank developed in the mid-1950s as an upgrade of the T-54. It featured improved NBC (nuclear, biological, chemical) protection, a more powerful 100 mm D-10T gun, and better engine performance, making it more versatile than its predecessor. With its balance of firepower, armor, mobility, and ease of mass production, it became the backbone of Soviet and Warsaw Pact armored forces. The T-55 is the most produced tank in history, with tens of thousands built and exported worldwide. It saw combat in numerous Cold War and post-Cold War conflicts, and many remain in service or modernized even today.

T-62

The T-62 was a Soviet main battle tank introduced in 1961 as a successor to the T-55. It was the first tank in the world to be armed with a smoothbore 115 mm U-5TS gun, giving it superior armor penetration compared to contemporary NATO tanks. The T-62 featured improved frontal armor and a low profile but suffered from mechanical issues, a cramped interior, and a relatively slow rate of fire. It saw extensive combat in conflicts such as the Yom Kippur War, the Soviet–Afghan War, and the Iran–Iraq War. Although eventually replaced by more advanced designs like the T-72, the T-62 remained in service for decades and is still used in some regions today.

SU-122-54

The SU-122-54 was a Soviet self-propelled gun and tank destroyer developed in the early 1950s. Built on a lengthened T-54 chassis, it mounted a powerful 122 mm D-49 gun in a fixed casemate with limited traverse, designed primarily for engaging enemy armor at long range. It featured thick frontal armor, a low profile, and additional machine guns for close defense, making it one of the most heavily armed postwar Soviet SPGs. However, its role was increasingly taken over by turreted main battle tanks like the T-54/55 and later the T-62, which offered more flexibility in combat. Only around 77 units were produced, and it served in limited numbers until the late 1970s before being retired.

IT-1

The IT-1 (“Istrebitel' Tankov” or Tank Destroyer) was a Soviet missile tank introduced in the mid-1960s. Based on the T-62 chassis, it was equipped with a launcher for the 3M7 “Drakon” guided missile instead of a conventional tank gun. This gave it the ability to engage and destroy enemy tanks at ranges up to 3,300 meters with high accuracy, a revolutionary concept at the time. However, it could only fire guided missiles, had limited ammunition capacity, and suffered from reliability issues, which restricted its practicality. Produced in small numbers from 1968 to 1970, the IT-1 was soon withdrawn, but it represented an important step in Soviet experimentation with missile-armed armored vehicles.

T-64

The T-64 was a Soviet main battle tank introduced in the early 1960s, representing a huge leap in armored warfare technology. It was the first tank to combine composite armor, a smoothbore 125 mm gun, and an autoloader, which reduced the crew to three men instead of four. Despite its advanced design, it was mechanically complex and expensive, so it was not exported widely, unlike the T-55 and T-62. The T-64 formed the backbone of elite Soviet tank units during the Cold War and served as the technological basis for later designs such as the T-72 and T-80. Even today, upgraded T-64s remain in service, notably with Ukraine.

T-72

The T-72 was a Soviet main battle tank introduced in 1969 as a more practical and mass-producible alternative to the advanced but costly T-64. It carried a 125 mm smoothbore gun with an autoloader, composite armor, and could mount reactive armor later on, making it one of the most capable tanks of its era. Designed for simplicity and reliability, it was widely exported and became the backbone of many Warsaw Pact and allied armies. The T-72 has seen extensive combat worldwide, from the Iran–Iraq War to the Gulf War and more recent conflicts. With thousands still in service and many upgraded variants, it remains one of the most recognizable and widely used tanks in history.

2S1 Gvozdika

The 2S1 Gvozdika (“Carnation”) is a Soviet self-propelled howitzer introduced in the early 1970s. Built on a modified MT-LB armored chassis, it is fully amphibious and highly mobile, capable of keeping up with mechanized units. It mounts a 122 mm howitzer in a rotating turret, giving it full 360° traverse and the ability to deliver both direct and indirect fire. The Gvozdika has seen service in numerous conflicts, from Afghanistan to the Middle East, thanks to its versatility and ease of use. It remains in service with many countries, often upgraded with modern fire-control systems and improved ammunition.

T-80

The T-80 was a Soviet main battle tank introduced in 1976, designed as a high-performance successor to the T-64. It was the first production tank in the world powered by a gas turbine engine, giving it exceptional speed and acceleration compared to diesel-powered counterparts. Armed with a 125 mm smoothbore gun with an autoloader, it could fire a variety of ammunition, including guided missiles, and carried composite armor with later variants adding explosive reactive armor (ERA). While highly advanced, it was also expensive to produce and maintain, limiting its numbers compared to the more widely fielded T-72. The T-80 served as an elite tank in Soviet and later Russian forces, with upgraded versions like the T-80U and T-80BVM still in service today.

Black Eagle

The Black Eagle (Object 640) was a Russian prototype main battle tank developed in the 1990s by the Omsk Design Bureau. It was based on a lengthened T-80 chassis, featuring improved armor protection and a revolutionary new turret design. The turret was fitted with a separate armored bustle housing an autoloader and ammunition compartment, intended to greatly improve crew survivability compared to earlier Soviet designs where ammunition was stored inside the crew compartment. It was expected to carry a 125 mm smoothbore gun, with potential for a larger caliber upgrade. However, the project was canceled in the early 2000s due to lack of funding, and many of its ideas later influenced the design of the T-14 Armata.

1992-Present

T-90

The T-90 is a Russian main battle tank introduced in 1992 as a modernization of the T-72, incorporating features from the T-80. It is armed with a 125 mm smoothbore gun capable of firing anti-tank guided missiles, and protected by composite armor, Kontakt-5 explosive reactive armor (ERA), and Shtora-1 active protection systems. Designed to be more reliable and affordable than the T-80, it became Russia's primary battle tank of the post-Soviet era. The T-90 has been widely exported, serving in countries like India, Algeria, and Syria, with India producing its own licensed version, the T-90S “Bhishma”. Constantly upgraded, variants like the T-90M “Proryv” remain in active service today, making it one of the most important tanks in Russia's arsenal.

T-14 Armata

The T-14 Armata is Russia's latest main battle tank, first unveiled in 2015 during the Moscow Victory Day Parade. Built on the new Armata Universal Combat Platform, it features a revolutionary design with an unmanned turret and a crew of three housed in a heavily armored armored capsule within the hull for maximum protection. It is armed with a 125 mm smoothbore gun (with plans for a 152 mm upgrade), an autoloader, and the ability to fire guided missiles. The T-14 is protected by Malachit explosive reactive armor, an Afghanit active protection system, and advanced sensors, making it one of the most technologically ambitious tanks in the world. However, production has been limited due to cost and technical challenges, and only small numbers are currently in service with the Russian Army.

T-90M

The T-90M “Proryv” is the most advanced modernization of the T-90 main battle tank, introduced into Russian service in the late 2010s. It features a 125 mm 2A46M-5 smoothbore gun with improved accuracy, new ammunition types, and the ability to fire anti-tank guided missiles. Its protection is enhanced with Relikt explosive reactive armor (ERA), cage armor against RPGs, and an upgraded Kalina fire-control system with modern thermal sights and battlefield networking capability. The T-90M also has a more powerful engine, better crew ergonomics, and additional defenses against drones and modern threats. Considered the pinnacle of the T-90 family, it serves as one of Russia's frontline tanks alongside upgraded T-72s and T-80s.
