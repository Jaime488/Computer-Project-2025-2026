WW1 (1914-1918)

Little Willie

The Little Willie was the first prototype tank developed by Britain in 1915 during World War I. It was designed as a response to the stalemate of trench warfare, aiming to cross rough terrain and barbed wire. The vehicle had a boxy shape, riveted armor, and ran on a track system adapted from agricultural tractors. It was slow, reaching only about 3 km/h, and its design had many flaws, such as poor trench-crossing ability. Despite never seeing combat, it laid the groundwork for future British tanks like the Mark I.

Mother (HMLS Centipede)

The "Mother," officially known as HMLS Centipede, was the prototype built after Little Willie in 1915. It featured a rhomboid shape with extended tracks running around the entire hull, allowing it to cross trenches much more effectively. The design included sponsons on the sides where cannons or machine guns could be mounted, setting the pattern for early British tanks. Although it was still slow and mechanically unreliable, it solved key mobility issues that plagued Little Willie. This prototype directly influenced the creation of the Mark I, the world's first operational tank.

Mark I

The Mark I was the world's first operational tank, introduced by Britain in 1916 during World War I. It came in two variants: "Male," armed with two 6-pounder naval guns and machine guns, and "Female," armed only with machine guns. Its rhomboid shape and full-length tracks allowed it to cross trenches and rough terrain that stopped infantry and cavalry. The tank was slow, noisy, and mechanically unreliable, but it provided vital protection against small arms fire and barbed wire. Despite its flaws, the Mark I marked the beginning of modern armored warfare.

Mark II

The Mark II was a direct development of the British Mark I tank, built in early 1917. It was intended mainly as a training tank, since the Mark IV was already in development, but some were sent into combat due to urgent needs. The design remained almost identical to the Mark I, with the same rhomboid shape, sponsons, and armament options. Its armor was relatively thin, making it vulnerable to enemy fire, especially since German forces had begun using armor-piercing ammunition. Although not very successful in battle, the Mark II served as a stepping stone in tank evolution during World War I.

Mark III

The Mark III was a further development of the British rhomboid tanks, built in 1917 mainly for training purposes. It retained the overall design of the Mark I and II, with sponsons and full-length tracks, but incorporated improved armor layout and some internal changes. Unlike its predecessors, it was never intended for large-scale combat deployment. Only a small number were produced, and they were used to train crews while more advanced designs like the Mark IV were prepared. The Mark III helped smooth the transition toward better-protected and more reliable frontline tanks.

Mark IV

The Mark IV was the most widely produced British tank of World War I, introduced in 1917 as an improvement over earlier designs. It featured thicker armor, better fuel storage placement for safety, and more reliable mechanical systems. Like its predecessors, it came in "Male" versions with 6-pounder guns and "Female" versions with multiple machine guns. The Mark IV played a major role in the Battle of Cambrai, where tanks were used in mass for the first time with significant effect. Its balance of protection, firepower, and trench-crossing ability made it the backbone of Britain's armored forces during the later years of the war.

Mark V

The Mark V was a major upgrade of the British rhomboid tank, entering service in 1918. It introduced a new and more powerful Ricardo engine along with a single-driver epicyclic gearbox, which made it much easier to control compared to earlier models. The design maintained the "Male" and "Female" variants, with the former carrying 6-pounder guns and the latter armed with machine guns. It was more reliable and mobile on the battlefield, allowing for better coordination with infantry. The Mark V proved highly effective during the Allied offensives of 1918, especially at the Battle of Hamel and during the Hundred Days Offensive.

Medium Mark A "Whippet"

The Medium Mark A Whippet was a British tank introduced in 1917 to complement the heavier rhomboid tanks. Unlike the slow and bulky heavy tanks, the Whippet was designed for speed and mobility, reaching up to 13 km/h. It had a boxy hull mounted on a lengthened Holt tractor chassis and was armed with four 0.303-inch Hotchkiss machine guns. Its role was to exploit breakthroughs created by heavier tanks, pursuing retreating enemy forces and attacking infantry positions. The Whippet proved effective in this role, with some units achieving remarkable success during the final offensives of World War I.

Medium Mark B

The Medium Mark B was a British tank developed at the end of World War I as a successor to the Whippet. It had a longer, box-like hull with the engine in the rear and a central fighting compartment for better crew efficiency. Armed with multiple machine guns, it was intended for fast exploitation and infantry support rather than heavy breakthroughs. The design allowed better visibility and internal space but suffered from reliability issues and mechanical problems. Only a small number were built, and they saw little to no combat before the war ended.

Medium Mark C "Hornet"

The Medium Mark C, nicknamed Hornet, was a British tank designed in 1918 as an improvement over the earlier Medium Mark B. It had a longer hull with a better layout, placing the engine at the rear and giving the crew more space and protection. The tank was armed with a 57 mm 6-pounder gun in the front along with several machine guns for close defense. It was faster and more reliable than its predecessors, intended for mobile warfare and exploitation after heavy tanks broke through enemy lines. However, it was completed too late to see action in World War I, and only a limited number were produced before development shifted toward newer interwar designs.

Mark VII

The British Mark VII was a prototype heavy tank developed near the end of World War I as a further evolution of the rhomboid series. It was based on the Mark V but equipped with a new hydraulic transmission system to make driving and steering easier. The tank retained the long rhomboid hull with sponsons for 6-pounder guns and machine guns, but its experimental drivetrain proved unreliable. Only a handful were built, and they never saw combat before the war ended. The Mark VII served mainly as a testbed, influencing later British heavy tank development.

Mark VIII "Liberty"

The Mark VIII, also called the Liberty or International, was a heavy tank jointly developed by Britain, France, and the United States at the end of World War I. It was the largest of the rhomboid tanks, designed to improve on the Mark V with thicker armor, a longer hull, and a more powerful 300-hp Liberty engine. Its armament consisted of two 6-pounder guns mounted in side sponsons along with up to seven machine guns for all-around defense. The design aimed to standardize Allied tank production, but mass production was delayed and the war ended before it could be deployed. Around 100 were eventually built in the U.S. during the early 1920s, where they served mainly for training and testing rather than combat.

Mark IX

The Mark IX was a British tank developed in 1918 as the world's first purpose-built armored personnel carrier. It was designed to transport up to 30 infantry soldiers safely across no man's land, protecting them from small arms fire and shrapnel. The vehicle was long and boxy, with tracks running around the hull like other rhomboid tanks, but it had minimal armament—usually just a few machine guns. Ventilation was poor, and conditions inside were extremely cramped and uncomfortable for the troops. Only a small number were built, and they arrived too late to see combat in World War I, but the Mark IX set the groundwork for future APC concepts.

Interwar & WW2 (1919-1945)

Vickers Medium Mk I

The Vickers Medium Mark I was a British tank introduced in the mid-1920s as one of the first modern interwar designs. Unlike the rhomboid World War I tanks, it featured a rotating turret mounted on a conventional box-shaped hull, which became the standard layout for future tanks. It was armed with a 3-pounder (47 mm) gun and several machine guns, providing a balance of firepower for its time. The tank was lightly armored and mechanically unreliable, but it was fast and maneuverable compared to earlier heavy designs. Though never used in combat, it played a crucial role in training and in shaping British armored doctrine leading into World War II.

Vickers Independent

The Vickers Independent was a British experimental heavy tank built in 1926. It had a distinctive design with a large central turret mounting a 3-pounder gun and four smaller machine gun turrets placed around it, making it one of the earliest examples of a multi-turreted tank. The tank was large and imposing, with a crew of eight, but its size and complexity made it difficult to produce and maintain. Only one prototype was ever built, and it never went into mass production. However, its concept strongly influenced other nations' designs in the 1930s, including the Soviet T-35 and the German Neubaufahrzeug.

Matilda I

The Matilda I, officially known as the Infantry Tank Mk I, was a small British infantry tank introduced in the late 1930s. It was lightly armed with only a single .303 Vickers machine gun, as its main role was to support infantry rather than fight enemy armor. The tank had very thick armor for its size, which made it resistant to most German anti-tank weapons during the early stages of World War II. However, it was extremely slow and had poor cross-country mobility, limiting its battlefield usefulness. The Matilda I saw action in France in 1940 but was soon withdrawn and replaced by more capable designs like the Matilda II.

Matilda II

The Matilda II, officially the Infantry Tank Mk II, was a British infantry tank that entered service in 1939 as a major improvement over the Matilda I. It was armed with a 2-pounder (40 mm) gun in a fully rotating turret, giving it much greater offensive capability. The tank was heavily armored for its time, making it almost invulnerable to most German anti-tank weapons during the early campaigns, especially in France and North Africa. Its main drawback was its slow speed and mechanical complexity, which limited mobility and ease of maintenance. Despite this, the Matilda II earned a strong reputation in the early years of World War II before being phased out by 1943 in favor of faster, more versatile tanks.

Cold War (1946-1991)

Centurion Mk 3

The Centurion Mk 3 was one of the most important postwar British tank variants, introduced in the late 1940s. It featured a new 20-pounder (84 mm) main gun, giving it far superior firepower compared to earlier models armed with the 17-pounder. The tank also had improved armor protection and a stabilized gun, which increased accuracy while on the move. With its balanced combination of firepower, mobility, and protection, the Centurion Mk 3 set the standard for modern main battle tanks. It became the backbone of British armored forces during the early Cold War and was widely exported to other nations.

Chieftain

The Chieftain was a British main battle tank introduced in the 1960s, designed to replace the Centurion. It was armed with the powerful 120 mm L11 rifled gun, which at the time was one of the most effective tank guns in the world. The tank featured heavily sloped armor for increased protection and was equipped with advanced fire control systems for its era. However, its engine was underpowered and often unreliable, which limited its mobility compared to some contemporary designs. Despite this, the Chieftain served as the backbone of British armored forces for decades and was widely exported, influencing later designs like the Challenger series.

Challenger 1

The Challenger 1 was a British main battle tank introduced in the early 1980s as a successor to the Chieftain. It featured advanced Chobham composite armor, offering exceptional protection against both kinetic and chemical energy rounds. Armed with the 120 mm L11A5 rifled gun, it was capable of firing a wide variety of ammunition types with high accuracy. While well-protected, it suffered from reliability and mobility issues due to its aging engine and suspension system. The Challenger I served with the British Army during the Gulf War, where it proved highly effective in combat, before being replaced by the Challenger 2 in the late 1990s.

1992-Present

Challenger 2

The Challenger 2 is the current main battle tank of the British Army, introduced in 1998 as a successor to the Challenger 1. It is armed with a 120 mm L30 rifled gun and features advanced Chobham/Dorchester armor, providing outstanding protection against modern anti-tank threats. The tank combines firepower, mobility, and survivability, making it one of the most heavily protected tanks in the world. It has been deployed in combat operations, including in Iraq and Afghanistan, demonstrating its effectiveness in both urban and open-field warfare. Continuous upgrades to its electronics, communications, and defensive systems keep the Challenger 2 relevant on the modern battlefield.

Ajax

The Ajax is a next-generation British armoured reconnaissance vehicle developed by General Dynamics UK to replace the aging CVR(T) family. It is armed with a 40 mm CTAS40 cannon, a 7.62 mm coaxial machine gun, and can be fitted with anti-tank missiles. Powered by a 600 kW MTU engine, it can reach speeds up to 70 km/h and uses torsion bar suspension for mobility. The vehicle has modular armor for protection against small arms and shrapnel, with variants accommodating between three crew members to six personnel. Development has faced challenges, including noise and vibration issues, but limited trials continue with initial service expected by 2025.
